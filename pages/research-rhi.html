Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT) in rhizobia

The relationships between bacterial symbionts and their eukaryotic hosts in different ecological contexts are critical drivers of evolution that can impact multiple biological levels: their genomes, cellular processes, traits of the organisms involved, their population structure, and even speciation. At the molecular level, HGT is one of bacteria's most important mechanisms and sources of genomic variations. Since bacteria are very prone to experiencing HGT, bacterial symbionts can acquire alleles from other populations or genes from other bacterial species that can impact the relationship with their hosts. As a postdoc in Plant Biology and GEMS (the symbiosis institute) at UIUC, I have been working on understanding the effect of HGT on the genome and population structure of S. meliloti using phylogenetic-dependent and -independent approaches to detect and analyze HGT in natural bacterial populations. S. meliloti has a multipartite genome composed of three replicons (chromosome, pSymA, and pSymB) with different evolutionary histories. Our results show extensive HGT among them across strains. Also, the characteristics of the events (e.g., genomic physical distribution, potential gene loss after the transference) vary among replicons likely due to their different origin.
