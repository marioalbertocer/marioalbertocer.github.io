Changes in genome architecture associated with gene family evolution

Most of our knowledge on the occurrence and fate of gene duplicates (paralogues) comes from studies of whole genome duplications in plants, animals, fungi, and a few model organisms from other clades. The more we know about protists, the more we realize the complexity of the genome architecture and its importance for the evolution of gene families. I became interested in this topic after developing a tool (PhyloChromoMap) to map the phylogenetic history of genes in eukaryotic chromosomes (Cerón-Romero et al. 2018). Using this tool, I displayed the extent of the ectopic recombination that P. falciparum experiences in its subtelomeres to generate diversity in antigenic gene families. Later, I supervised an exceptional undergraduate student in a project recently submitted for publication (Martínez-Eraso, et al. 2023) in which she explores the importance and evolution of this mechanism through the whole Plasmodium genus. I am interested in expanding this type of exploration to understand antigenic gene family evolution in other protists parasites (e.g., other Apicomplexans and Excavates) and karyotype evolution in other lineages including plants and fungi.

Examples of phylogenomic chromosome mapping in four species of Plasmodium. This approach maps the phylogenetic history of genes into the chromosomes resulting in a "profile" of gene conservation that allows the detection of young regions. The large subtelomeric young regions with a high density of antigenic genes (lines below the bars/chromosomes) are signatures of frequent (ectopic) subtelomeric recombination to generate antigenic diversity. This process is more intense in P. yoelii and P. falciparum (Martínez-Eraso, et al. 2023).
