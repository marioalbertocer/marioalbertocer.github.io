Phylogenomic Chromosome Mapping

I have been working on developing a phylogenomic chromosome mapper (i.e., PhyloChromoMap)1, and developing the third version of our phylogenomic pipeline (i.e., PhyloTOL). PhyloChromoMap is a tool that maps the evolutionary history of genes in chromosomes of eukaryotic organisms. PhyloTOL contains a collection of ~13000 homolog genes that include up to ~1000 lineages including bacteria, archaea and eukaryotes. I am currently working to deploy these methods to 1) infer the root of the eukaryotic tree of life and 2) integrate with PhyloChromoMap to depict the absolute strand polarity in Kinetoplastids.

Karyotype evolution in Plasmodium falciparum

We used PhyloChromoMap and our bioinformatic pipeline to generate a phylogenomic map of the chromosomes of P. falciparum strain 3D7. We estimated the level of conservation of 2116 genes along the chromosomes and identified ‘young’ regions (i.e. those with recent or fast evolving genes) that are enriched in subtelomeric regions as compared to internal regions. We also demonstrated that the patterns of molecular evolution for paralogous genes differ substantially depending on their location as younger paralogs tend to be found in subtelomeric regions and older paralogs in internal regions. Combining these observations with analyses of synteny we demonstrated that subtelomeric regions are actively shuffled among chromosome ends, which is consistent with the hypothesis that these regions are prone to ectopic recombination2. We demostrated (through comparing dN/dS ratios) that the distribution of paralogs from most gene families is guided by selection except in gene family var. The mechanism of karyotype evolution of P. falciparum that we study exemplifies that eukaryotic genomes are more dynamic than traditionally thought.

Figure 2. Paralogs in (a) subtelomeric regions of Plasmodium falciparum 3D7 tend to be young whereas paralogs in (b) internal regions tend to be old. Timing Gene Duplications/Losses Across the Eukaryotic Tree of Life

Although gene duplications are important drivers of genome evolution and novelty3,4, the dynamics of gains and losses of gene duplications through the evolutionary history of eukaryotes remains poorly understood. I am using PhyloTOL to compare the rates of gains and losses of ancient vs. recent gene duplications, and test at least two hypotheses: 1) rampant gene duplications occurred early in the history of eukaryotes followed by gradual loses, and 2) episodic gene duplications have been occurring during the history of the eukaryotes, followed by gradual losses. The finding of this project will contribute to understand the role of duplications in processes such evolution of gene families and speciation.

On the right, episodic gene duplications have been occurring during the history of the eukaryotes (yellow stars and numbers with different colors), followed by gradual losses. Phylogenetic history of photosyntesis

The history of photosynthesis is linked to the acquisition of plastids. The popular view is that the ancestor of Archaeplastida acquired its plastid (i.e., primary plastid) from Cyanobacteria5. Consequently, archaeplastida lineages must be nested within Cyanobacteria in the phylogeny of all nuclear photosynthetic genes. However, some nuclear-encode photosynthetic genes reflect other origins within bacteria. This could be due to either poor resolution of single gene trees, interdomain lateral gene transfer or primary plastid acquisition by a “shopping bag” mechanism6,7. The shopping bag model argues that there were many transient endosymbionts that helped the establishment of the primary plastid6,7. Under this model, we expect archaeplastida lineages to be frequently nested within clades of different cyanobacterial lineajes or different bacterial lineajes. In order to tackle this goal, I am using PhyloTOL to test which of these mechanisms explains better the evolution of all nuclear photosynthetic genes in eukaryotes.

A) Simple scenario: Genes came from a unique endosymbiotic event involving a cyanobacterial lineage and the ancestor of Archaeplastida. B) Shopping bag: Genes came from multiple endosymbiotic events involving multiple cyanobacterial lineajes and the ancestor of Archaeplastida. C) Shopping bag: Genes came from multiple endosymbiotic events involving multiple bacterial lineajes and the ancestor of Archaeplastida.
