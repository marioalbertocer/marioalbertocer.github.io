Timing gene duplications and losses in eukaryotic evolution

Gene duplication and gene loss are the more pervasive type of gene rearrangement in eukaryotic evolution and there is evidence of their profound impact on the diversity of many major eukaryotic clades such as Viridiplantae, Metazoa, and Fungi. Considering this, I led a study to find the root of the phylogenetic tree of eukaryotes with an approach that minimizes the number of duplications and losses needed to explain the discordance between gene trees and the species tree (Cerón-Romero et al. 2022). Our results pointed to a root between Opisthokonta and the rest of the eukaryotes. However, we also determined that understanding the evolutionary aspects that led to the striking differences in genome size between Metazoa and Fungi is important for validating our proposed root. I am interested in studying the effect of gene duplications and losses in the evolution of eukaryotes by detecting their occurrence and decay (gene loss) in all the branches of the tree. This study will be crucial to understand the earliest events in eukaryotic evolution.

Examples of two hypotheses that can be tested in this study: A) Rampant duplications occurred early in the evolution of eukaryotes followed by gradual gene loss, and B) Rampant duplications occurred episodically in the history of eukaryotes followed by gradual gene loss.
